Withdrawal Strategy Monte Carlo

Will your money last through retirement?

Monte Carlo withdrawal simulator — compare strategies, model inflation, and stress-test your retirement income plan

Most retirement calculators show you one scenario — the average. But markets don’t deliver average returns every year. This simulator runs thousands of possible futures based on historical return patterns, showing whether your withdrawal strategy can survive the full range of market outcomes. For educational and informational purposes only — not financial advice. See disclaimer.

Portfolio Settings
More simulations = smoother results but slower. 1,000 is a good default.
Withdrawal Strategy
Same dollar amount each year (not adjusted for inflation). Simplest approach — purchasing power decreases over time.
Withdrawal increases annually with inflation to maintain purchasing power. This is the classic “4% rule” approach.
Withdraw a fixed percentage of current portfolio value each year. Withdrawal amount varies with portfolio performance — you’ll never fully deplete, but income fluctuates.
Dynamic strategy with a floor and ceiling. Starts with a base withdrawal, increases if portfolio grows significantly, decreases if it drops.
If portfolio grows beyond ceiling trigger above starting value, increase withdrawal. If it drops below floor trigger, decrease withdrawal.
Inflation is simulated as a random variable each year, not a fixed deduction. Historical US: ~3% mean, ~1.5% std dev.
Simulation Mode
Draws random returns each year from a normal distribution using historical mean & standard deviation.
Randomly samples actual historical annual returns. Captures real crashes, booms, and fat tails that statistical models miss.
Int'l Stocks data starts in 1970. Years before that will use US Stocks for the international allocation.
Assumptions
US Stocks: 10.2% mean, 15.5% std dev
Intl Stocks: 8.5% mean, 17.0% std dev
Bonds: 5.0% mean, 5.5% std dev
Cash: 3.0% mean, 1.0% std dev

Based on long-term historical nominal annual returns. US Stocks reflect S&P 500 data from 1926–2024 (Ibbotson/Morningstar SBBI). Int’l Stocks use MSCI EAFE from 1970–2024. Bonds reflect US intermediate-term government bonds (1926–2024). Cash reflects 3-month Treasury bills (1926–2024).
