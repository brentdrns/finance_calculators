Investment Strategy Monte Carlo

What could your portfolio actually look like?

Monte Carlo portfolio simulator — compare asset allocations, model inflation, and stress-test your retirement plan

Most growth calculators show you one line — the average. But markets don’t deliver average returns every year. This simulator runs thousands of possible futures based on historical return patterns, showing the full range of outcomes for different asset allocations. Portfolio Settings
More simulations = smoother results but slower. 1,000 is a good default.
Simulations run up to 60 years. Each path is checked for when it first crosses your target.
Contribution Growth
Your contribution stays the same dollar amount each year. In real terms, its purchasing power shrinks over time due to inflation.
Your contribution increases each year to keep pace with inflation, maintaining the same real purchasing power. This is the most realistic default for most savers. Requires “Adjust for inflation” to be enabled — otherwise contributions stay fixed.
Your contribution grows at a fixed annual rate you specify — useful for modeling expected salary raises (e.g., 3–5% per year).
Inflation is simulated as a random variable each year, not a fixed deduction. Historical US: ~3% mean, ~1.5% std dev.
Simulation Mode
Draws random returns each year from a normal distribution using historical mean & standard deviation.
Randomly samples actual historical annual returns. Captures real crashes, booms, and fat tails that statistical models miss.
Int'l Stocks data starts in 1970. Years before that will use US Stocks for the international allocation.
Assumptions
US Stocks: 10.2% mean, 15.5% std dev
Intl Stocks: 8.5% mean, 17.0% std dev
Bonds: 5.0% mean, 5.5% std dev
Cash: 3.0% mean, 1.0% std dev

Based on long-term historical nominal annual returns. US Stocks reflect S&P 500 data from 1926–2024 (Ibbotson/Morningstar SBBI). Int’l Stocks use MSCI EAFE from 1970–2024. Bonds reflect US intermediate-term government bonds (1926–2024). Cash reflects 3-month Treasury bills (1926–2024).

The simulator does not replay actual historical years. Instead, it draws random returns each year from a normal distribution using these parameters — so each simulation is a plausible but unique path.
